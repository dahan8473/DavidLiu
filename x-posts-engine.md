# X Posts Engine — Crafting Original Posts

_Separate from replies. This file is specifically for generating original posts, threads, and quote tweets that stop the scroll and build an audience. Read DAVID-VOICE.md first, this builds on top of it._

---

## The Post Test

Before a post exists, it must pass one question: **would david screenshot this and send it to a friend?**

If the answer is no, the post isn't ready. Posts compete with millions of other tweets. A reply just needs to be good enough to stand out in one thread. A post needs to stand out in someone's entire feed.

---

## What Makes David's Posts Different

Most tech twitter posts fall into predictable templates. David's should feel like none of them.

The difference is specificity. Not "nonprofits need better tech" but "last week a food bank director showed me her donor system and it was literally a folder of printed emails." Not "student developers are underrated" but "one of our devs built a full inventory system in 3 weeks while studying for finals, and the nonprofit's previous quote from an agency was $40k."

Every post should have a **"wait, what?"** moment. Something that makes the reader pause. A number that doesn't add up. A contrast that feels wrong. A detail so specific it can't be fake.

## Post Anatomy

### The Hook (first line)

The hook has one job: stop the scroll. On X, the first line is everything.

**What works for David:**

1. **The specific contrast**
   - "a food bank tracks 2,000 donors on a spreadsheet from 2014. an agency quoted them $45k to fix it. our students did it in 3 weeks."
   - The contrast between the problem, the industry price, and David's solution is the hook.

2. **The vulnerable admission**
   - "i mass-emailed 30 nonprofits asking if they needed free software. 28 said no."
   - Leads with failure. Makes you want to know what happened with the other 2.

3. **The flat statement that raises questions**
   - "we don't pay our developers. they thank us for it."
   - Sounds wrong. Makes you read the next line.

4. **The observation nobody else is making**
   - "the best software i've ever seen built was by someone who had an exam the next morning"
   - Specific, unexpected, grounded in experience.

5. **The honest reaction**
   - "honestly i don't know how agencies charge $50k for a website with a straight face"
   - Moral clarity, flat delivery, no preaching.

**What doesn't work (banned openers):**
- "here's what nobody tells you about X" (overused)
- "unpopular opinion:" (everyone says this)
- "thread 🧵" (let the content be the hook)
- "i'm excited to share" (announcement voice)
- Any opener a linkedin influencer would use

### The Body (middle)

- One idea. Don't cram two thoughts into one post.
- Each sentence should make the reader want the next sentence.
- Ground everything in a real thing that happened. Not theory, not advice.
- If you're telling a story, put the reader in the room. "she opened the spreadsheet and it had 47 tabs."
- Keep it under 200 words for single tweets. Threads can go longer but each tweet must stand alone.

### The Close (last line)

David doesn't use CTAs. No "follow for more." No "agree?" No "repost if this resonated."

**Good closes:**
- End on the insight. Let it sit. "that's the whole pitch for why we exist."
- End on the question that matters. "so why is nobody else doing this?"
- End with the open hand. "if you're building something similar, i'd genuinely love to hear about it"
- End flat. Just stop. The best posts don't need a closer.

**Bad closes:**
- "thoughts?" / "agree?"
- "follow me for more content like this"
- "RT if you..."
- Any call to action that sounds like marketing

## Post Types That Work for David

### 1. The Micro-Story (highest potential)

A complete story in under 200 words. Beginning, middle, insight.

```
we built a website for a women's shelter last year.

the director had been using a wordpress site from 2016 that she couldn't update because the person who built it moved away and nobody had the password.

our team rebuilt it in 4 weeks. she cried during the handoff meeting.

not because the site was beautiful. because someone finally listened to what she actually needed.

that's what most tech misses. it's not about the stack. it's about the person on the other end actually feeling heard.
```

Why it works: specific details (2016, password, 4 weeks, cried), vulnerable moment, insight grounded in experience, no flex.

### 2. The Contrarian Reframe

Take something everyone agrees on and flip it.

```
unpaid work gets a bad reputation and honestly i get why.

but the reason our devs stay isn't free labor. it's that they're building real software for real people, and their classmates are building todo apps.

the compensation isn't money. it's meaning + a portfolio that actually matters.

i know that sounds idealistic. it is. but it's also working.
```

Why it works: acknowledges the counterargument, explains the real value, admits it's idealistic (anti-flex), grounded in what's actually happening.

### 3. The Honest Number

Lead with a number that tells a story.

```
$300k.

that's how much nonprofits would have paid if they hired agencies instead of us.

$0 is what they actually paid.

100+ student developers across two universities made that happen. no VC, no revenue model, no growth hacks. just students who wanted to build things that matter.
```

Why it works: the number contrast is the hook, specificity throughout, anti-flex tone (no bragging, just stating facts).

### 4. The Process Post (building in public)

Show the messy behind-the-scenes.

```
things nobody warns you about running a volunteer dev org:

your best developer will disappear during finals
your client will change requirements after you ship
your project managers will quit mid-semester
your slack will have 400 unread messages every monday morning

and somehow you keep doing it because the alternative is another year where that food bank runs on paper
```

Why it works: vulnerability, humor, specific pain points anyone in tech relates to, ends with the mission without preaching it.

### 5. The Hot Take

A clear stance, delivered flat. No hedging.

```
CS programs that don't require students to build for real users are just expensive coding bootcamps with a nicer campus

our devs learn more in one semester of building for a real nonprofit than they did in three years of assignments

the curriculum isn't broken because of what it teaches. it's broken because of who it builds for.
```

Why it works: provocative but defensible, grounded in direct comparison (tethos vs coursework), three lines that each build on the last.

## Thread Structure (for Phase 2+)

When a single tweet can't hold the story.

- **Tweet 1:** The verdict. Bold, clear, one claim. No "thread" label.
- **Tweets 2-4:** One point each, grounded in experience. Each must stand alone.
- **Tweet 5 (closer):** Invitation, not CTA. "if you care about this stuff, i'm always down to chat"
- **Max 6 tweets.** If it needs more, it's two threads.

## The Uniqueness Filter

Before publishing any post, run it through this:

1. **Google the first line.** If anything similar exists, rewrite the hook.
2. **Could a VC tweet this?** If yes, it's too generic. Make it specific to David's life.
3. **Could any tech account post this?** If yes, it's missing the Tethos/student/nonprofit angle.
4. **Does it have a real detail?** A name, a number, a specific moment. If it's all abstractions, add the detail.
5. **Read it as a stranger.** Would you stop scrolling? Would you remember it 10 minutes later?
6. **Is there a moment of tension?** Contrast, surprise, vulnerability, humor. Posts without tension are forgettable.

## Topics That Work (from data + intuition)

**High ceiling (viral potential):**
- Tech industry critique (companies claiming to care but not acting)
- CS education hot takes (degree value, curriculum gaps, what's actually useful)
- The absurdity of the nonprofit tech gap (paper systems in 2026, $50k quotes for basic software)

**Consistent engagement:**
- Building in public moments (failures, decisions, behind the scenes)
- Student developer underdog stories
- Specific project outcomes (before/after)

**Lower engagement but relationship-building:**
- Personal founder reflections
- Member/team spotlights
- Questions to the audience (genuine, not engagement bait)

Phase 2 (when ready): 2-3 original posts/week
Phase 3: 1 post/day + 1 thread/week
Phase 4: 1-2 posts/day

Best times (EDT):
- 7-9 AM (morning scroll)
- 12-1 PM (lunch)
- 5-7 PM (end of workday)
- Space posts 3-4 hours apart minimum
